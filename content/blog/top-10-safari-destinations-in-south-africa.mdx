South Africa is the best entry point to African safari — excellent
infrastructure, self-drive options, malaria-free reserves, and world-class
private concessions. These ten are the standouts.

## 1. Kruger National Park

One of the great wildlife parks on Earth — vast, Big Five, and uniquely
accessible by self-drive or guided lodge.

## 2. Sabi Sand Game Reserve

Private concessions bordering Kruger, world-famous for close, reliable leopard
sightings.

## 3. Madikwe Game Reserve

A large malaria-free Big Five reserve near the Botswana border — ideal for
families.

## 4. Pilanesberg National Park

Big Five in an extinct volcanic crater, malaria-free and close to Johannesburg.

## 5. Addo Elephant National Park

Hundreds of elephants in the Eastern Cape, malaria-free and pairing with the
coast.

## 6. Phinda Private Game Reserve

Seven habitats in KwaZulu-Natal, strong for cheetah and rare species.

## 7. Hluhluwe-iMfolozi Park

The oldest proclaimed park in Africa and the great rhino conservation success
story.

## 8. Kgalagadi Transfrontier Park

Red Kalahari dunes, black-maned lions, and raptors — a wild, remote
classic.

## 9. Marakele National Park

Dramatic mountain scenery with Big Five in the Waterberg.

## 10. Tswalu Kalahari

South Africa's largest private reserve — meerkats, pangolin, and desert
exclusivity.

## How South African safari works

Three models: self-drive in national parks (cheapest, very doable in Kruger and
Addo), guided lodge stays in national parks, and all-inclusive private
concessions with off-road game drives and walking. Choose by budget and how
hands-held you want it; South Africa uniquely supports all three.

## When to go

The dry winter (May–September) is prime: sparse vegetation and animals
concentrated at water. Summer (November–March) is lush, green, great for birds
and newborns but harder viewing. Malaria-free reserves remove a major planning
concern, especially for families.

## Safety and etiquette

Stay in the vehicle unless a guide says otherwise, keep quiet and still at
sightings, never feed or crowd animals, and follow malaria precautions where
relevant. Choose lodges with genuine conservation and community credentials.
