A few safari destinations are not just good — they are wildlife phenomena, the
places that define what "safari" means. These are the icons.

## Serengeti, Tanzania

The stage of the Great Migration — over a million wildebeest moving in an
endless cycle, with the predators that follow them.

## Maasai Mara, Kenya

The Serengeti's northern continuation, famous for big cats and the dramatic
Mara River crossings.

## Okavango Delta, Botswana

A river that floods into the Kalahari, creating a seasonal water-world explored
by mokoro canoe — pristine, exclusive, extraordinary.

## Ngorongoro Crater, Tanzania

A collapsed volcanic caldera holding one of the densest concentrations of
large animals on Earth — a natural amphitheatre of wildlife.

## Kruger, South Africa

One of the great parks of the world and the most accessible Big Five
destination anywhere.

## South Luangwa, Zambia

The home of the walking safari and superb leopard country.

## Chobe, Botswana

Astonishing dry-season elephant and buffalo concentrations along the river.

## Etosha, Namibia

A vast salt pan where wildlife gathers at waterholes against a surreal white
landscape.

## Bwindi, Uganda

Not savanna at all — mountain forest where you trek to habituated wild
gorillas, one of wildlife travel's peak experiences.

## Hwange, Zimbabwe

Huge elephant herds and classic Kalahari-sand wilderness.

## What makes a destination impressive

Sometimes it is sheer numbers in motion (the Serengeti migration), sometimes a
landscape that concentrates life (Ngorongoro, the Okavango), and sometimes a
single profound encounter (Bwindi's gorillas). The greatest safaris fuse
spectacle with the feeling of genuine wilderness.

## Visiting the icons responsibly

These places are under pressure from their own fame. Choose operators that
limit vehicle numbers, never bait or crowd animals, hold strict permits where
required (gorilla trekking is tightly capped for good reason), and reinvest in
conservation and communities. The wildlife spectacle survives only while the
ecosystems and the people protecting them do.
