A safari is a once-in-a-lifetime trip for most people, and the planning
decisions — season, circuit, operator — matter more than for almost any other
journey. Here is the order to make them.

## Step 1: Choose the season first

Season outranks everything. Dry season concentrates animals at water and thins
the bush for visibility; specific events (the Great Migration crossings) have
narrow windows. Decide what you want to see, find its peak month, and build the
whole trip inside that band.

## Step 2: Build a logical circuit

Open the [map](/), filter to your country, and group parks into a sensible
circuit rather than a scatter. Classic routes exist for a reason — the
Kenyan Mara–Nakuru–Amboseli loop, Kruger and its private reserves. Combine
contrasting habitats for variety, not five versions of the same plain.

## Step 3: Decide the trip style

Self-drive (cheapest, flexible, mainly South Africa), guided lodge (comfort
plus expert guiding), or all-inclusive private concession (off-road, walking,
night drives, premium). Match this to budget and experience honestly; it
shapes cost and the whole feel.

## Step 4: Vet operators and lodges hard

This is the decisive ethical and quality step. Choose operators that cap
vehicles per sighting, never bait or crowd animals, employ professional
guides, and channel real money into conservation and local communities. Read
recent, specific reviews. The cheapest option often means crowded, unethical
sightings.

## Step 5: Handle health and documents early

Check malaria status of each area and arrange prophylaxis where needed; confirm
required vaccinations (yellow fever for some routings), visas, and travel
insurance that covers remote evacuation. Do this as soon as the route is fixed,
not last.

## Step 6: Plan the daily rhythm

Safari days are bookended: a pre-dawn game drive, midday rest in the heat, and
a late-afternoon drive into golden light. Build the itinerary around two short,
intense windows and do not over-schedule transfers — long road days waste game
time.

## Step 7: Pack purposefully

Binoculars (non-negotiable — one pair per person), neutral-coloured clothing,
warm layers for cold dawn drives, sun protection, a good camera with zoom, and
any medication. Soft duffel bags for light-aircraft transfers' strict weight
limits.

## Step 8: Behave for the wildlife

Stay in the vehicle, quiet and still at sightings, never pressure a guide to
approach or bait, and keep distance — especially at migration crossings and
with predators on a kill. The animals' wellbeing is the trip.

## Put it together

Season, then circuit, then style, then a hard-vetted operator, then health.
Open the [map](/), draw the route, and a safari becomes the trip of a lifetime,
done right.
