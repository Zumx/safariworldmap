Mountain gorilla (Gorilla beringei beringei, IUCN Endangered) tracking is
among the most compelling wildlife encounters on earth. It is also one of the
most tightly regulated, for good reason: the entire wild population numbers
around 1,000 individuals, split between the Virunga Massif shared by Uganda,
Rwanda, and the DRC, and Bwindi Impenetrable National Park in Uganda. Every
tracking group is a potential disease vector, and the rules are there to
protect the animals before the experience.

## Bwindi Impenetrable National Park, Uganda

Permit price: USD 800 per person per trek (as of 2024, set by Uganda Wildlife
Authority). Bwindi holds roughly half the world's mountain gorilla population
across four sectors — Buhoma, Ruhija, Rushaga, and Nkuringo — each with
multiple habituated groups. The diversity of terrain means that Bwindi
treks range from a relatively short walk in Buhoma to a multi-hour steep
climb in Ruhija or Nkuringo. Rushaga has the most habituated groups, making
it the sector with the highest permit availability at any given time.

Booking is through Uganda Wildlife Authority directly or through licensed
Ugandan tour operators. Permits sell out months in advance for peak season
(June to September, December to February). You are guaranteed one hour with
the gorillas once the trackers locate the group; the trackers go ahead on the
day and radio back position. Group size is capped at eight trekking visitors
per habituated group per day, plus guides and trackers.

## Volcanoes National Park, Rwanda

Permit price: USD 1,500 per person per trek (Rwanda Development Board,
2024 pricing). Rwanda's pricing is a deliberate strategy: by charging a
premium and capping group numbers, Rwanda limits total annual visitor
footprint while maximising revenue per permit. It works. Rwanda's gorilla
sector funds a highly professional ranger corps, park infrastructure across
Volcanoes and Nyungwe, and community benefit programmes in the Kinigi buffer
zone. The Volcanoes sector of the Virunga Massif is also the location of the
Karisoke Research Center, founded by Dian Fossey in 1967.

Fossey spent 18 years in the Virungas studying mountain gorillas at close
range, demonstrating habituation was possible and documenting social structures
that fundamentally changed how the animals are understood. She was murdered
in 1985. The Dian Fossey Gorilla Fund now manages Karisoke and an
associated research station, and much of what guides know about gorilla
behaviour and health management derives from Karisoke's decades of data.

Rwanda's Volcanoes gorilla treks depart from Kinigi, typically a 90-minute
drive from Kigali. Hike times vary from 30 minutes to five hours depending
on which group is allocated and where it moved overnight. Several luxury
lodges — Bisate Lodge (Wilderness), Singita Kwitonda, One&Only Gorilla's
Nest — sit within a short drive of the park gate, and a full-day "gorilla
experience" permits are also available at USD 3,000 for extended time with
the researchers.

## Virunga National Park, DRC

Permit price: USD 400 per person per trek. Virunga covers a vast area of
eastern DRC including the Mikeno sector where habituated gorilla groups
live. The permit is the cheapest of the three countries for mountain gorilla
tracking, and the experience — when conditions allow — is extraordinary, set
against the dramatic backdrop of Nyiragongo volcano. The security caveat is
real and cannot be understated. Virunga has been intermittently closed to
tourism due to armed group activity in eastern DRC since the 1990s, and parts
of the park have seen significant conflict. As of 2024, the situation remains
volatile. Check current FCO/State Department travel advisories within days of
departure, not weeks. African Parks, which manages Virunga, maintains a
professional ranger corps, but no ranger corps can guarantee visitor safety
during active conflict. The Mikeno Lodge, inside the park, offers an
immersive base when the area is accessible.

## Rules That Apply Everywhere

One hour is the maximum permitted viewing time with any gorilla group,
regardless of country or permit tier. This limit is enforced by the tracker
and guide — when the hour is up, trekkers move away. Trackers begin timing
from when the group is reached, not from when the trek began.

Maximum group size is eight trekking visitors per gorilla group per day.
This is non-negotiable under International Union for Conservation of Nature
guidelines adopted by all three countries. Guides and trackers are additional
and do not count toward the eight.

Health rules are strict. Visitors with any respiratory illness, fever, or
gastrointestinal symptoms must not trek — gorillas have no immunity to human
respiratory diseases including common colds, and multiple deaths of gorillas
from human-transmitted illness have been documented. You will be asked at the
briefing. Answer honestly. Surgical masks are required to be worn within
7 metres of any gorilla; in practice guides insist on masks throughout the
approach once the group is close. Flash photography is prohibited — the sudden
light disturbs and can alarm animals, particularly silverbacks. The minimum
distance is 10 metres; in practice gorillas move where they wish, and groups
do occasionally approach or pass near trekkers. The instruction is to hold
still, avoid direct eye contact with the silverback, and let the trackers
manage the situation.

Do not eat, drink, or smoke within sight of the gorillas. Remove your waste
and all packaging from the forest.

## Logistics and Preparation

Pack light but pack for full-day effort: hiking boots with ankle support,
waterproof jacket, long trousers, gardening or leather gloves (vegetation
grabs), a walking pole, and two litres of water. Bwindi and Virunga treks
can involve crawling through dense vegetation and steep scrambles in rain.
Altitude ranges from 1,500 to 2,500 metres. At Volcanoes NP you ascend from
around 2,600 metres; altitude tiredness is possible for some visitors.

Porter hire is available at all three parks and is strongly recommended.
Porters are community members who carry bags and assist on difficult terrain.
The fee is typically USD 15-20 and represents genuine income for families
in areas where conservation has replaced other land uses.

## The Conservation Impact of Permit Revenue

Mountain gorilla permit revenue is not merely tourism income — it is the
primary financial mechanism sustaining the protected areas and ranger forces
that prevented gorilla extinction in the 20th century. Rwanda's USD 1,500
permit model funds the Rwanda Development Board's entire Volcanoes NP
operational budget, the community benefit programme at Kinigi, and a
contribution to the International Gorilla Conservation Programme. Uganda's
USD 800 permits fund Uganda Wildlife Authority ranger salaries, patrol
equipment, and veterinary capacity including a gorilla health monitoring
team that provides remote veterinary care to habituated groups.

The mountain gorilla population, declared Critically Endangered for decades,
was upgraded to Endangered by the IUCN in 2018 — the first great ape to
recover its status. The population increase from roughly 620 in 2008 to over
1,000 in 2018 is directly attributable to the intensive protection that
permit revenue funds. This is one of the clearest available demonstrations
that high-value, low-volume wildlife tourism can pay for genuine conservation
outcomes when the revenue is appropriately managed.

## Combining Gorilla Tracking With Other Wildlife

The standard Rwanda trip — Kigali arrival, two nights at Volcanoes NP (one
gorilla permit, one golden monkey tracking), return to Kigali — takes three
to four days. A more complete trip adds Akagera National Park (two nights)
for the Big Five savanna experience and optionally Nyungwe NP (two nights)
for chimpanzee tracking and the canopy walk. This Rwanda combination — gorilla,
golden monkey, chimpanzee, Big Five — is unmatched in its diversity for a
country of Rwanda's size. The Uganda equivalent adds Kibale's chimpanzees
and Queen Elizabeth's tree-climbing lions to Bwindi's gorillas.
