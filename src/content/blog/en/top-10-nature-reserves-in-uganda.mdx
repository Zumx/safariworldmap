Uganda is one of the most biodiverse countries on earth relative to its size,
sitting at the meeting point of East African savanna, West African equatorial
forest, and the Albertine Rift. The country holds half the world's mountain
gorilla population, the highest chimpanzee density in Africa, and a savanna
park system that is undervisited relative to Kenya and Tanzania. Permits and
standards are managed by Uganda Wildlife Authority.

## 1. Bwindi Impenetrable National Park, Western Uganda

The single most important primate destination in East Africa. Bwindi holds
roughly half the world's mountain gorilla (Gorilla beringei beringei, IUCN
Endangered) population across four sectors — Buhoma (north), Ruhija (east),
Rushaga (south), and Nkuringo (southwest). Forty-two habituated groups exist
across all sectors; each admits eight trekkers per day at USD 800 per permit.
The gorilla habituation experience, available in Rushaga, allows six visitors
to spend four hours with a group in the early stages of habituation — more
intimate than a standard trek and at USD 1,500 closer to the Rwandan price.
The forest is ancient — 25,000 years old — and botanically extraordinary,
with 120 mammal species and more than 350 bird species including 23 Albertine
Rift endemics. Best season: June to August and December to February.

## 2. Mgahinga Gorilla National Park, Western Uganda

The smallest of Uganda's national parks, Mgahinga protects the Ugandan
portion of the Virunga Massif and shares the volcanic landscape with
Rwanda's Volcanoes NP and the DRC's Virunga NP. One habituated gorilla
group (the Nyakagezi group) is resident; it has occasionally crossed into
Rwanda's territory, making permit certainty at this park lower than Bwindi.
Golden monkey (Cercopithecus kandti, IUCN Endangered) tracking is available
and is one of the best-value wildlife experiences in Uganda. The Batwa
community lived inside the Virungas for millennia before gazettement; the
Mgahinga Batwa Cultural Trail preserves elements of their forest culture
and is an important community benefit programme.

## 3. Queen Elizabeth National Park, Western Uganda

Uganda's most visited national park and the most wildlife-diverse, with
several distinct ecosystems — the Kasese sector savanna, the Mweya Peninsula,
the Kyambura Gorge, and the Ishasha sector in the south. The tree-climbing
lions of Ishasha, which rest in fig trees on the open savanna in a behaviour
shared only with a small Lake Manyara population in Tanzania, are the
flagship experience. The Kazinga Channel connecting Lakes Edward and George
holds the highest density of hippopotamus (Hippopotamus amphibius, IUCN
Vulnerable) in Uganda. Boat safaris on the channel are exceptional for birds.
Chimpanzee in the Kyambura Gorge are habituated for tracking.

## 4. Murchison Falls National Park, Northern Uganda

Uganda's largest national park, spanning the point where the Victoria Nile
forces through a 7-metre gap in the Rift Valley escarpment. The boat safari
up the Nile from Paraa to the base of the falls is among the best wildlife
experiences in East Africa — hippo, Nile crocodile (Crocodylus niloticus),
African buffalo, and the Nile Delta forest birds are seen at very close
range. The Buligi peninsula north of the Nile holds good savanna game
including lion, elephant, and giraffe. The Rothschild's giraffe (Giraffa
camelopardalis rothschildi, IUCN Endangered) population here is one of
the most important remaining. Best season: January to February and June
to August.

## 5. Kibale National Park, Western Uganda

The chimpanzee capital of Africa. Kibale holds an estimated 1,500 chimpanzee
(Pan troglodytes schweinfurthii, IUCN Endangered) in one of the highest
densities of any forest in Africa. The habituated Kanyanchu community admits
morning and afternoon tracking groups at Uganda Wildlife Authority rates.
Kibale also holds 13 other primate species including red colobus, grey-
cheeked mangabey, and red-tailed monkey — a morning forest walk can produce
five species before breakfast. The park is contiguous with Queen Elizabeth
National Park to the south via the Kyambura corridor.

## 6. Kidepo Valley National Park, Northeastern Uganda

The most remote of Uganda's major parks and, for those who make the effort
to get there, the most rewarding. Kidepo sits in the Karamoja region on
the South Sudan and Kenya borders, accessible by an eight-hour road journey
from Kampala or by scheduled charter flight to Apoka airstrip. The park
holds cheetah (Acinonyx jubatus, IUCN Vulnerable) — rare elsewhere in East
Africa — as well as lion, elephant, buffalo, and a bird list of more than
475 species. The Narus Valley is the wildlife heart; Apoka Safari Lodge
the primary accommodation. The Karamojong community manages a cultural
village adjacent to the park.

## 7. Lake Mburo National Park, Western Uganda

The most accessible of Uganda's wildlife parks from Kampala, Lake Mburo
is a four-hour drive on the Masaka Road. It is Uganda's only park with
impala, zebra, and eland, and the only one where you can walk and cycle
through wildlife country. The park's several lakes hold hippo and waterbirds.
The horseback safari offered by Mihingo Lodge is one of the few places in
East Africa where big game can be approached on horseback at genuinely close
range. No lion or elephant in the park makes walking straightforward and
less tense.

## 8. Rwenzori Mountains National Park, Western Uganda

The "Mountains of the Moon" are a UNESCO World Heritage Site covering the
Rwenzori range, which straddles the Uganda-DRC border. The range is the
source of the Nile's headwaters and holds a unique montane ecosystem: giant
lobelia, giant groundsel, heath forest, and afroalpine moorland up to the
permanent ice and rock of Margherita Peak (5,109m). It is a trekking rather
than a game-drive destination; three-day and six-day circuits are operated
by Rwenzori Trekking Services and Rwenzori Mountaineering Services. Wildlife
includes the rare Rwenzori three-horned chameleon (Trioceros johnstoni).

## 9. Semuliki National Park, Western Uganda

Semuliki protects the Ugandan extension of the Ituri Forest in the DRC and
is among the most accessible lowland equatorial forest in East Africa.
The park's wildlife list is more Central African in character than Ugandan:
forest buffalo, pygmy hippopotamus (Hexaprotodon liberiensis, IUCN
Endangered), and forest elephant. The Sempaya hot springs — including the
famous female spring whose geyser erupts on a regular cycle — are a
geological feature visited alongside bird watching. The bird list includes
species found nowhere else in Uganda, with strong representation of West
African forest species.

## 10. Bigodi Wetland Sanctuary, Western Uganda

Not a national park but a community-managed wetland sanctuary bordering
Kibale National Park, Bigodi is run by the Kibale Association for Rural
and Environmental Development (KAFRED) and is among the most successful
community wildlife enterprises in Uganda. The two-hour guided swamp walk
produces red-tailed monkey, grey-cheeked mangabey, black-and-white colobus,
and abundant waterbirds including the great blue turaco, all on community
land with community guides whose income depends directly on the wildlife.
Entry fees are modest; the model is widely cited as a template.

## Planning a Uganda Safari

The classic Uganda circuit runs: Kampala — Kibale (chimps) — Queen Elizabeth
(Kazinga boat, Ishasha tree-climbing lions) — Bwindi (gorilla) — fly or
drive back. Allow at least seven to nine days for this circuit. Kidepo is
a separate trip requiring a full day of travel each way or a charter flight.
